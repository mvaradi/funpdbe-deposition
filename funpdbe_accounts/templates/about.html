FunPDBe

Community driven enrichment of PDB data with structural and functional annotations

The FunPDBe project aims to establish an integrated and easily accessible resource of structural and functional annotations for macromolecular structure data in the Protein Data Bank (PDB). It is a collaboration between the Protein Data Bank in Europe (PDBe) and world-leading UK-based providers of structural bioinformatics data. The project promotes interoperability, comparative analysis and exchange of structural and functional annotations by implementing common data standards and infrastructure to collect these enhanced annotations. The project aims to significantly increase the impact of structural data globally by implementing a central sustainable data resource and a uniform data access mechanism (via FTP and REST API) for distribution of these valuable functional and structural annotations, developed by researchers in the UK. The data will also be made accessible via web interface by developing re-usable web components.

Background

Protein Data Bank in Europe (PDBe [1]) is one of the founding members of the worldwide Protein Data Bank (wwPDB) [2] and manages the single worldwide macromolecular structure archive, the Protein Data Bank (PDB) [3], in collaboration with its partner sites. PDBe manages deposition and annotations of PDB submissions originating from Europe and Africa. PDBe works closely with its wwPDB partners to develop deposition and annotation policies and infrastructure.

The model coordinates available in the PDB provide unique information including details of secondary, tertiary and quaternary structure in addition to information on residue interactions, interface residues and atomic level details of interactions between small molecules and a given macromolecule.

However, the atomic coordinates only possess biological value when they are supplemented by information providing biological context and enriched by value-added annotations [4]. When combined with biological knowledge and context, the coordinates can provide mechanistic information on biological processes. Presently, the PDB annotation includes the following main elements: information on secondary structure; basic data on quaternary structure; binding site environment; cross-referencing information for each polypeptide chain to the corresponding entry from UniProt and, if applicable, enzyme commission numbers; and NCBI taxonomy IDs for organism information. The individual ‘small molecule’ chemical entities present in PDB entries are described in a separate reference resource called the chemical component dictionary [5].

It is difficult to exploit to the full extent the rich data available in the PDB not only because of the complexity of the data itself, but also due to the limited and static nature of the annotations provided by the PDB and most importantly because the biological context, structural and functional annotations pertaining to each entry are almost completely lacking [6, 7].

FunPDBe is designed to provide a solution that will bring together the basic annotations from the PDB archive, the value-added annotations that are available at PDBe and the currently fragmented structural and functional annotations from the numerous structural bioinformatics data resources in the UK such as: catalytic sites (Catalytic Site Atlas – CSA [8]), enzyme reaction mechanisms (Mechanisms in Enzymes – MACiE [9]), analysis of biological assemblies (3D Complex [10]), predicted functional site data for ligand binding sites or interface residues (CATH- Gene3D FunSites [11]; 3DLigandSite [12]; POPSCOMP [13]), nucleolar signal localisation detector (NOD [14, 15]), the predictor of O-GlcNAc Sites (POGSPSF, in preparation) and the JABAWS2 webservices framework that integrates predictors of intrinsic disorder and conservation [16]. The community has also developed a number of algorithms to predict the effect of mutations or variants on the structure and/or function of macromolecules [17-22].

Objectives

Our objective is to provide FunPDBe, a comprehensive resource of functional annotation for structures in the PDB, by combining the work and expertise of 10 leading structural computational biologists in the UK. We develop the processes necessary to facilitate the provision of functional annotations alongside existing PDB information by integrating value-added data from specialist data resources into the PDBe infrastructure. Focus is on the following three major areas:

  • Prediction of functional sites including binding and interface sites, led by C Orengo
  • Annotation of ‘known’ functional sites in proteins, especially enzymes, ligand binding sites and protein interfaces, led by J Thornton
  • Predicting the structural effects of mutations and variants, which is a critical area for translational research, led by M Sternberg

In each of the above areas a number of groups from the UK bioinformatics community participate as collaborators to provide functional annotations.

Why is it relevant?

From the early 1970s when the PDB was established, structural data have contributed valuable insights into the function of biological macromolecules. This is evident from the number of downloads of PDB entries (more than 500 million each year), and from the steady number of citations that each PDB entry receives, which shows a sustained use of this information [23]. Recent analysis by wwPDB partners shows that more than 25% (119 out of 425) of the databases published in the NAR database issue between 2011 and 2016 use PDB information and provide valuable annotations based on structural data. This analysis also points to the lack of a central repository that would provide structural and functional annotation alongside the basic PDB annotation. Such fragmentation of information and lack of data standards also makes it difficult to carry out large-scale data analysis.

The basic data in the PDB currently include atomic coordinates, experimental data, information about data collection and some basic annotation that can be easily derived from the atomic coordinates (e.g., secondary structure). However, many bioinformaticians have developed valuable niche resources as part of their on-going research (e.g., the ‘Catalytic Site Atlas’ that catalogues enzyme catalytic residues from the literature).

By bringing together functional annotations available in these different data resources in a central repository, FunPDBe will support the wider research community in its efforts to exploit macromolecular structure data to derive knowledge. Standardisation of the metadata and the availability of multiple data access mechanisms (REST API, multiple file formats on FTP site) will also support large scale data analysis by expert users, as they will not have to implement ad hoc mechanisms to access each individual data resource, an effort which is often performed independently by each researcher.

The project will also improve sustainability, ensuring that the annotations are archived safely, are accessible for the foreseeable future, remove duplication of effort and thus protect the work and investment that has gone into developing these specialised data resources. FunPDBe will become a unique, open global resource, and help secure the UK’s leading role in structural computational biology into the future.

No other resource integrates curated known functional residues with predicted functional residues from diverse resources to more comprehensively identify active sites and interfaces, nor combines multiple predictions on the structural and functional impacts of genetic variations. By developing standards and combining predictions from multiple methods, the FunPDBe project will provide more comprehensive and more reliable data than any individual resource and to a much larger set of users than currently enjoyed by any of the individual resources.

What do we provide?

The participating bioinformatics teams have developed a number of algorithms and niche data resources that provide structural and functional annotations. The annotation information available in these resources is widely used as evident from the number of citations and/or by the number of users accessing these data. PDBe is one of the core EMBL and Elixir data resources and has more than 550K unique users visiting its website annually. The team has also developed the SIFTS data resource [24] and data analysis tools (PISA [25] 3143 citations in Web of Science, PDBeFOLD [26] 2071 citations) that are widely used. For instance, the latest release of Jalview (2.10, October 2016) [27] uses SIFTS to obtain the residue level cross-referencing between UniProt and the PDB. Jalview has over 70,000 regular users worldwide and is used in teaching and research across all biological disciplines.

FunPDBe provides the necessary infrastructure to bring together the basic annotation from the PDB archive, annotations from SIFTS and other PDBe resources and the value-added structural and functional annotations provided by the world-leading bioinformatics resources in the UK. The infrastructure provides a mechanism for the participating teams to update structural and functional annotation and keep the information up-to-date. It also establishes the necessary protocol for other bioinformatics teams from around the globe, not initially involved in this project, to contribute annotations to FunPDBe so these can be made available via the defined data standards thus creating a global, open source resource for structural and functional annotation for structural data. The major impact of this project is expected to come from combining the valuable knowledge from the niche annotations with the archival information from the PDB and making it accessible in a standardised way. By making these data available via the PDBe website and standardised REST API it can reach the wider user community that accesses the PDBe website in addition to the users that already access the individual data resources.

FunPDBe is a unique data resource that not only brings together the most valuable annotations but also helped to develop data standards to simplify the use of these data. The data standards and the central data resource developed in the project should allow standardised access helping the contributing teams to carry out comparative analysis and validation of predicted annotations and help improve the prediction algorithms by promoting interoperability. Additionally the new developments will simplify large-scale analysis of structural data and its exploitation for translational research. The implementation of REST API and access via FTP should make it possible for the wider user community to access these data in a straightforward fashion. To represent the data on web interfaces, web components are being developed and deposited in the BioJS repository [28] for wider distribution. These web components will standardise the way these data are visualised and will be integrated into PDBe web pages. The standardised access to the annotation information and the availability of the web components to visualise data will enable all the FunPDBe participants and other major data resources such as UniProt [29], InterPro [30] and Ensembl [31] to access these data and/or make them available to their users on their respective websites. The project will help individual researchers gain easy access to macromolecular structure data and the associated niche annotations and help ensure UK researchers stay internationally competitive. The easy access to structural data and annotations will support obtaining insights into the effects of genetic variations, development of new tools to aid synthetic biology, enhancement in valuable annotations to enrich information available for agriculturally important macromolecules and contribute to human health by aiding interpretation of gene variation.
